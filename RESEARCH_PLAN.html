A. Rationale

Background

Febrile illness is one of the most common oncological emergencies, affecting up to 80% of patients receiving chemotherapy for hematologic malignancies and 10-50% of those with solid tumors. While many febrile episodes resolve with prompt antimicrobial therapy, a subset of patients rapidly deteriorate and require intensive care unit (ICU) admission for hemodynamic support, mechanical ventilation, or management of multi-organ dysfunction.

Early identification of patients at high risk for ICU admission is clinically important for several reasons:

  • Improved outcomes: Prompt ICU referral in deteriorating patients is associated with improved survival
  • Resource optimization: Appropriate triage optimizes utilization in hospitals with limited ICU capacity
  • Care planning: Risk stratification enables tailored monitoring intensity and goals-of-care discussions

Gap in Knowledge

Current clinical scoring systems‚Äîincluding the Multinational Association for Supportive Care in Cancer (MASCC) score and quick Sequential Organ Failure Assessment (qSOFA)‚Äîwere not specifically designed to predict ICU admission and demonstrate variable performance in this clinical context. Machine learning approaches may capture complex, non-linear relationships among clinical variables and potentially outperform traditional scoring systems.

Significance & Societal Impact

This research addresses a critical clinical need in oncology practice. Accurate prediction of ICU needs would enable proactive resource allocation in resource-limited settings, support informed shared decision-making, potentially reduce mortality through earlier intervention, and decrease healthcare costs by optimizing ICU utilization.

B. Research Questions & Hypotheses

Primary Research Question

Can a machine learning model accurately predict ICU admission in oncology patients presenting with febrile illness using clinical variables available at initial presentation?

Secondary Research Questions

  1. Does the machine learning model outperform established clinical scores (MASCC, qSOFA)?
  2. Which clinical features are most predictive of ICU admission?
  3. Does model performance vary across patient subgroups (tumor type, neutropenia status, age)?

Hypotheses

H1

An XGBoost machine learning model incorporating multiple clinical features will achieve an AUROC ‚â• 0.80 for predicting ICU admission.

H2

The machine learning model will achieve superior discriminative performance (AUROC) compared to MASCC score alone and qSOFA score alone.

H3

Model performance will be consistent across patient subgroups defined by tumor type and neutropenia status.

Expected Outcomes

  • Development of a validated prediction model with good discrimination (AUROC > 0.80)
  • Identification of key clinical features driving ICU admission risk
  • Clinical utility assessment demonstrating positive net benefit across relevant decision thresholds
  • Publication-ready manuscript with model performance metrics and clinical interpretation
