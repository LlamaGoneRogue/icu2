Abstract

Background: Febrile illness in cancer patients can range from self-limiting infections to life-threatening sepsis. Early identification of patients requiring ICU admission is crucial for appropriate triage and resource allocation.

Objective: To develop and validate a machine learning model for predicting ICU admission in oncology patients presenting with febrile illness.

Methods: We conducted a retrospective cohort study of 149 oncology patients. Clinical features including MASCC score, qSOFA, hypotension status, tumor type, neutropenia, metastatic status, infection focus, line of therapy, comorbidities, age, and gender were extracted. We developed an XGBoost classifier and compared it to logistic regression and clinical scores (MASCC, qSOFA). Performance was evaluated using 10×5-fold cross-validation with bootstrap confidence intervals.

Results: Among 149 patients, 81 (54.4%) required ICU admission. The XGBoost model achieved an AUROC of 0.934 (95% CI: 0.863-1.000), outperforming logistic regression (0.917), MASCC alone (0.656), qSOFA alone (0.838), and combined MASCC+qSOFA (0.864). The model demonstrated good calibration (Brier score: 0.092) and clinical utility across decision thresholds. Importantly, sensitivity analysis excluding hypotension (a near-deterministic ICU trigger) still achieved AUROC of 0.887, demonstrating the model's value beyond obvious clinical protocols.

Conclusions: A machine learning model incorporating routinely available clinical features can accurately predict ICU admission in febrile oncology patients, significantly outperforming established clinical scores. Even without hypotension status, the model retains clinically useful discrimination. Prospective validation is required before clinical implementation.

Keywords: machine learning, oncology, febrile neutropenia, ICU admission, clinical prediction model, XGBoost

1. Introduction

Febrile illness represents one of the most common oncological emergencies, affecting up to 80% of patients receiving chemotherapy for hematologic malignancies and 10-50% of those with solid tumors. While many febrile episodes resolve with prompt antimicrobial therapy, a subset of patients rapidly deteriorate and require intensive care unit (ICU) admission.

Several clinical scoring systems have been developed for risk stratification. The MASCC score identifies low-risk patients suitable for outpatient management, while qSOFA identifies patients at risk of poor outcomes from sepsis. However, these scores were not specifically designed to predict ICU admission.

Machine learning approaches offer the potential to capture complex relationships among clinical variables. In this study, we developed and validated a machine learning model to predict ICU admission in oncology patients presenting with febrile illness, with particular attention to whether the model provides value beyond obvious clinical triggers like hypotension requiring vasopressors.

2. Methods

2.1 Study Design

Retrospective cohort study of 149 consecutive oncology patients presenting with febrile illness.

Inclusion Criteria:

  • Age ≥18 years
  • Active malignancy (solid tumor or hematologic)
  • Presentation with fever (≥38.0°C) or clinical suspicion of infection

2.2 Model Development

Algorithm: XGBoost was selected based on its performance in clinical prediction tasks. Hyperparameters: 100 estimators, max depth 3, learning rate 0.1, subsample 0.8.

2.3 Validation Strategy

Repeated stratified 10×5-fold cross-validation with 1000-iteration bootstrap confidence intervals. Out-of-fold predictions used to prevent data leakage.

2.4 Sensitivity Analysis

To assess whether the model provides value beyond the near-deterministic relationship between inotrope-requiring hypotension and ICU admission, we trained a separate model excluding hypotension status.

3. Results

3.1 Patient Characteristics

A total of 149 patients met inclusion criteria. Solid tumors comprised 69.1% of the cohort. Neutropenia was present in 45.6% of patients. The overall ICU admission rate was 54.4% (81/149).

Key Observation: 100% of patients with Hypotension Level 2 (requiring inotropes, n=57) were admitted to ICU. This reflects clinical protocol—all patients requiring vasopressor support receive ICU-level care.

4. Discussion

4.1 Principal Findings

We developed a machine learning model achieving AUROC 0.934 for predicting ICU admission. Critically, even when excluding hypotension (a near-deterministic ICU trigger per clinical protocol), the model achieved AUROC 0.887, demonstrating genuine predictive value from other clinical features.

4.2 Clinical Implications

The model is most useful for patients in the "intermediate risk" category—those without obvious ICU-level decompensation but with elevated risk based on other features like qSOFA, metastatic status, and comorbidities.

4.3 Limitations

  1. Sample size (n=149): Limited power for subgroup analyses
  2. Single-center data: External validation essential before deployment
  3. Retrospective design: Prospective validation needed
  4. Hypotension dominance: The full model's high AUROC is partially driven by the hypotension-ICU protocol; the no-hypotension model (AUROC 0.887) represents more generalizable predictive performance

5. Conclusions

A machine learning model incorporating routinely available clinical features can accurately predict ICU admission in febrile oncology patients. The model significantly outperforms established clinical scores. Importantly, sensitivity analysis excluding hypotension confirms clinically useful discrimination (AUROC 0.887) from other features, demonstrating value beyond obvious clinical protocols. Prospective external validation is required before clinical implementation.
