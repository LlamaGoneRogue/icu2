Machine Learning Prediction of ICU Admission in Febrile Oncology Patients

XGBoost-Based Clinical Decision Support for Early Risk Stratification

Background & Problem

The Clinical Challenge: Febrile illness is one of the most common oncological emergencies, affecting up to 80% of chemotherapy patients. While many episodes resolve with antibiotics, some patients rapidly deteriorate and require intensive care.

Why Early Prediction Matters:

  • Prompt ICU referral in deteriorating patients improves outcomes
  • Appropriate triage optimizes limited ICU bed utilization
  • Risk stratification enables tailored monitoring and goals-of-care discussions

Current Tools Are Inadequate:

  • MASCC Score: Designed for outpatient triage, poor at ICU prediction (AUROC ~0.65)
  • qSOFA: General sepsis marker, not oncology-specific (AUROC ~0.84)
  • Neither was validated for predicting ICU admission in cancer patients
Research Question: Can machine learning using routinely available clinical features predict ICU admission more accurately than existing clinical scores?

Methods

Study Design: Retrospective cohort study of consecutive oncology patients presenting with febrile illness at a tertiary cancer center.

Patient Population (n=149):

  • Solid tumors: 69% (n=103) | Hematologic: 31% (n=46)
  • ICU admission rate: 54.4% (81 patients)
  • Median age group: 51-60 years

Clinical Features (10 variables):

  • Severity: MASCC score, qSOFA, Hypotension level (0-2)
  • Disease: Tumor type, Metastatic status, Neutropenia
  • Clinical: Infection focus, Line of therapy, Comorbidities
  • Demographics: Age group, Gender

Model Development:

  • Algorithm: XGBoost Classifier (max_depth=3, n=100, lr=0.1)
  • Validation: 10√ó5-fold stratified cross-validation (50 folds)
  • Confidence intervals: 1000 bootstrap iterations
  • Comparators: Logistic regression, MASCC alone, qSOFA alone

Sensitivity Analysis: Trained separate model excluding hypotension to test if model provides value beyond obvious clinical triggers.

Discussion

Why XGBoost Outperforms Clinical Scores:

  • Captures non-linear interactions between variables
  • Optimally weights multiple risk factors simultaneously
  • Better specificity (88.2% vs 32.4%) reduces false alarms

Clinical Relevance:

  • High sensitivity (87.7%) ensures few high-risk patients are missed
  • High specificity prevents ICU over-triage
  • Model most valuable for intermediate-risk patients without obvious hemodynamic instability
  • Can serve as triage tool at emergency presentation

The Hypotension Question: Hypotension requiring vasopressors nearly always leads to ICU admission ‚Äî this is expected. The sensitivity analysis proves the model captures additional predictive signal from other variables.

Limitations

  • Sample size (n=149): Limits power for detailed subgroup analyses; wide CIs in smaller subgroups
  • Single-center data: May not generalize to other institutions or patient populations
  • Retrospective design: Subject to selection bias and missing data
  • No external validation: Model requires testing at independent sites before deployment
